​4. Technical & Audit Governance
​Human Auditor Integration: The system's robotic swarm behavior is transparent to the occupant. The Architect serves as the ultimate "Behavior Auditor," scoring robotic decisions to ensure they remain "human enough" and ethically aligned.
​Environmental Stewardship: Constant monitoring of global environmental metrics to trigger iterative upgrades to printed structural materials.

### **2. The "Dual-Front" Operations Protocol**
You need the house to be "two-faced": internally focused on life, externally focused on remediation.
 * **Internal Core (The Coil)**: The Service Bots and 2 of the Drones stay "inward-facing." Their priority is maintaining the Faraday grid, the Ark-Core plants, and the internal environment. They **never** leave unless the structural integrity of the coil drops below a 90% threshold.
 * **External Scavengers (The Easement Team)**: The Scavenger Bots and 4 Drones work the riverbed. They function on a **"Shift Rotation"**:
   * **Phase 1: Search & Tag**: Drones map the easement for plastic, metal, and organic debris.
   * **Phase 2: Extraction**: Scavenger Bots retrieve materials, sorting them at the Intake Hub.
   * **Phase 3: Processing**: The Central Core processes the waste. If the riverbed is "clean," the Scavenger Bots transition into **"Soil Remediation"** mode—planting the ARK species to stabilize the riverbank.
### **3. The "Easement Strategy" (The Worst-Case Site)**
Placing the core against an easement and a dry riverbed is brilliant—it provides a constant stream of "raw material" (the trash/debris left by the receding water) and gives you access to the water table once you hit it.
 * **Why the Easement is the "Best" Worst Site**:
   * **Raw Resource Flow**: The dry riverbed acts as a conveyor belt of materials.
   * **Easement Protection**: By occupying the easement, you define the "buffer zone," allowing the house to act as a barrier that prevents further erosion.
   * **Structural Anchoring**: The Cenote Coil’s helical design is excellent for riverbank stabilization; the "columns" act like deep roots, gripping the soil while the house grows upward.
